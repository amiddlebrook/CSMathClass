Section 1

Binary Trials (Success/Failure)

The simplest random experiments have two outcomes: 0 or 1.

Bernoulli(p)

X \sim \text{Bern}(p) \implies P(X=1)=p, P(X=0)=1-p E[X] = 1(p) + 0(1-p) = p Var(X) = E[X^2] - E[X]^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p)

Binomial(n, p)

A sum of $n$ independent Bernoulli trials. "Number of heads in $n$ flips."

X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p) P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} E[X] = E[\sum I_i] = \sum E[I_i] = np \quad (\text{Linearity}) Var(X) = \sum Var(I_i) = np(1-p) \quad (\text{Independence})

Hypergeometric(N, K, n)

Like Binomial, but without replacement. (e.g., Drawing 5 cards from a deck, how many are Aces?)

If population $N$ is much larger than sample $n$, Hypergeometric $\approx$ Binomial.

Section 2

Counts & Waiting Times

Geometric(p)

Number of Bernoulli trials until first success.

P(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p E[X] = 1/p Var(X) = (1-p)/p^2

Memoryless Property: knowing you've failed 10 times doesn't change the probability of succeeding on the 11th.

Negative Binomial(r, p)

Trials until $r$ successes.

Sum of r Geometrics E[X] = r/p Var(X) = r(1-p)/p^2

Poisson(λ)

The Law of Rare Events: If $n \to \infty$ and $p \to 0$ such that $np = \lambda$, Binomial$(n,p)$ converges to Poisson$(\lambda)$.
P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} E[X] = \lambda Var(X) = \lambda

Used for: radioactive decay, customer arrivals, typos on a page.

Section 3

Continuous Basics

Uniform(a, b)

Maximal entropy (randomness) on a finite interval.

f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a} \quad \text{for } x \in [a,b] E[X] = \frac{a+b}{2}, \quad Var(X) = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}

Exponential(λ)

Model for "time until next event" in a Poisson process.

f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \quad (x \geq 0) F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} E[X] = 1/\lambda, \quad Var(X) = 1/\lambda^2

Memoryless: $P(X > s+t | X > s) = P(X > t)$. Used for component lifetime modeling (assuming no aging).

Gamma(α, λ)

Generalization of Exponential. Sum of $\alpha$ independent Exponentials. Models "time until $\alpha$ events".

Beta(α, β)

Defined on $[0,1]$. Used to model probabilities (e.g., "what is the probability that this coin is biased?"). The "Conjugate Prior" for the Binomial distribution.

Section 4

The Normal (Gaussian) Family

The "Bell Curve" is the limit of sums of random variables (Central Limit Theorem).

f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}

Standard Normal Z ~ N(0,1)

Any Normal $X$ can be transformed to $Z$:

Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}

Key Intervals

  • $\pm 1\sigma$: 68.27%
  • $\pm 2\sigma$: 95.45%
  • $\pm 3\sigma$: 99.73%
  • $\pm 1.96\sigma$: 95.00% (Exact)
Why $\sqrt{2\pi}$? It comes from the Gaussian integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}$. Proved by squaring the integral and using polar coordinates!
Section 5

Python Analysis

Python Exploring distributions with SciPy 
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# 1. Poisson approximation of Binomial
n, p = 1000, 0.005
binom = stats.binom(n, p)
pois = stats.poisson(mu=n*p) # lambda = 5

print("P(X=3):")
print(f"Binomial: {binom.pmf(3):.5f}") # 0.14028
print(f"Poisson:  {pois.pmf(3):.5f}")  # 0.14037 (Very close!)

# 2. Waiting times (Exponential)
# Mean wait = 10 mins. Prob wait > 20 mins?
wait_dist = stats.expon(scale=10)
prob = 1 - wait_dist.cdf(20)
print(f"\nP(Wait > 20) = {prob:.4f}") # e^-2 ≈ 0.1353

# 3. Normal intervals
norm = stats.norm(loc=100, scale=15) # IQ distribution
top_1_percent = norm.ppf(0.99)
print(f"\nTop 1% IQ threshold: {top_1_percent:.1f}")
