Section 1

The Algorithm

Richard Feynman was the "Great Explainer". His method:

  1. Choose a concept.
  2. Explain it to an imaginary 12-year-old.
  3. Identify where you get stuck (your gaps).
  4. Return to the source material to fill gaps.
  5. Simplify and create analogies.

The Crayon Analogy: If you can't explain a concept to someone using only simple words and a box of crayons, you don't actually understand the concept. You're just repeating the "paints" and "brushes" of the experts without knowing how to draw the picture yourself.
Section 2

Inability to Explain is Ignorance

If you use jargon like "homomorphism" or "tensor" without defining it simply, you might be hiding your own lack of understanding.

Jargon is a compression algorithm for experts. When learning, you must decompress it.

üîç
The Searchlight Analogy: Jargon is like a narrow searchlight in the dark. It illuminates one specific spot perfectly, but if you don't know where you are in the forest, that spot doesn't help. The Feynman Technique is like turning on the **overhead lights**: you see the whole room, the floor, and the walls, even if the light isn't as intense.
Section 3

Power of Analogy

Voltage is like water pressure.

Eigenvectors are the axes of rotation.

Entropy is the number of yes/no questions to guess the state.

Good analogies bridge the known to the unknown.

üåâ
The Bridge Analogy: A new mathematical concept is like an island across a wide river. Formulas are the ferry boat‚Äîthey get you there, but they are slow and you might miss the boat. An analogy is a **bridge**. Once it's built, the island becomes part of your mainland, and you can cross over anytime with zero effort.

Why This Matters in CS

Code Reviews and Documentation: The Feynman Technique is the secret to being a Senior Engineer. A Junior can write code that a computer understands. A Senior writes code that a **human** understands. If you can't explain your system design simply in a README, your architecture is likely over-engineered and brittle. Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication.

Section 4

Learn in Public

The best way to force yourself to teach is to write online.

  • Start a technical blog.
  • Answer questions on StackOverflow.
  • Write Twitter threads explaining papers.

This not only solidifies your knowledge but builds your career capital.
