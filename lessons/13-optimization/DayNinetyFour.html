Section 1

Your Brain is for Thinking, Not Holding

David Allen (GTD): "Your mind is for having ideas, not holding them."

You cannot remember every theorem, proof, and technique in this 120-day course. You need an external system.

üèóÔ∏è
The Scaffolding Analogy: Your biological brain is like the cement of a building. It's strong once it dries, but it can only support so much weight at once. Your Second Brain is the **scaffolding**. It holds the structure in place while the cement cures, and allows you to reach heights (complex theories) that you could never climb alone.
Section 2

Atomic Notes (Zettelkasten)

Don't write long summaries. Write Atomic Notes. One concept per note.

Structure: 1. Concept Name 2. Definition (in your own words) 3. Example 4. Links to related concepts.
üß±
The LEGO Analogy: Traditional note-taking is like gluing LEGO blocks together into a big, finished model. It's hard to change. Atomic notes are like keeping the blocks **separate but sorted**. You can snap any two pieces together whenever you find a new connection, building something entirely new.

Tools: Obsidian, Roam Research, Notion.

Section 3

The Lemma Notebook

Keep a specific list of "Tricks of the Trade" or "Lemmas".

  • "When you see a sum, think Integral Test."
  • "To bound a probability, try Markov or Chebyshev."
  • "To show a matrix is PSD, check eigenvalues."

Review this list before starting any problem set.

Section 4

Spaced Repetition

For fundamental definitions that you MUST memorize, use Spaced Repetition Systems (SRS) like Anki.

Create flashcards: Front = "Definition of Convolution", Back = Formula. Review daily.

üå±
The Garden Analogy: A Second Brain is not a library; it's a **garden**. You "plant" ideas (seedlings), you "weed" out things that are no longer useful, and you "prune" notes to make them clearer. Most importantly, ideas "cross-pollinate" when they are linked together.

Why This Matters in CS

Caching and Indexes: Your biological brain has limited "RAM" (working memory) and slow "Disk" (long-term retrieval). A Second Brain is like an External Cache or a Search Index (like Elasticsearch). Instead of scanning your entire brain for a formula, you look at your Lemma Notebook, which points you directly to the data you need.

Section 5

Python: Knowledge Graph

Python Graph Visualization 
import networkx as nx
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Simulate Zettelkasten Links
topics = [
    ("LinAlg", "Calculus"),
    ("Calculus", "Optimization"),
    ("LinAlg", "Optimization"),
    ("Probability", "Stats"),
    ("Stats", "Optimization"), # ML
    ("Algorithms", "Graph Theory"),
    ("Graph Theory", "LinAlg") # Spectral Graph Theory
]

G = nx.Graph()
G.add_edges_from(topics)

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
pos = nx.spring_layout(G, seed=42)
nx.draw(G, pos, with_labels=True, node_color='lightblue', 
        node_size=2000, font_size=10, font_weight='bold')
plt.title("Your Knowledge Graph")
plt.show()
