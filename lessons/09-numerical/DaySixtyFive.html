Section 1

Laurent Series

If `f` has a singularity at `a` (e.g., `1/z`), we can't use Taylor series. We use Laurent Series:

f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n (z-a)^n

The coefficient `c_{-1}` is called the Residue, `\text{Res}(f, a)`.

Section 2

Residue Theorem

This is the fundamental theorem of integration technique.

Theorem: `\oint_\gamma f(z) \, dz = 2\pi i \sum \text{Res}(f, a_k)`

The integral over a closed loop depends ONLY on the sum of the residues of the singularities inside the loop.

Section 3

Application: Real Integrals

We can solve hard Real integrals by "closing the contour" in the complex plane.

Example: `\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{1+x^2} dx`.

  • Consider `\oint \frac{1}{1+z^2} dz` over a semicircle.
  • Singularities at `z = \pm i`. Only `i` is inside the upper semicircle.
  • Residue at `i` is `1/(2i)`.
  • Result: `2\pi i \cdot (1/2i) = \pi`. Matches `\arctan(\infty) - \arctan(-\infty)`.
Section 4

Conformal Maps

A holomorphic bijective map `f: U \to V` is a Conformal Map.

It preserves angles. These maps are used in engineering (fluid flow, electrostatics) to transform complex geometries into simple ones (like a disk).

Solving Laplace's equation `\Delta u = 0` is invariant under conformal maps!

üó∫Ô∏è Simple: The "Rubber Sheet" Analogy.
‚Ä¢ Imagine drawing a grid on a rubber sheet. A Conformal Map stretches and twists the sheet, but locally, every tiny grid square stays a square (it doesn't shear into a diamond).
‚Ä¢ This means if two lines cross at 90¬∞ on the original sheet, they still cross at 90¬∞ on the stretched sheet.
‚Ä¢ Engineers love this: You can solve air flow around a simple cylinder, then "map" it to an airplane wing shape, and the physics (angles of flow) stays correct!
Section 5

Riemann Mapping Theorem

Theorem: Any non-empty simply connected open proper subset of `\mathbb{C}` is conformally equivalent to the open unit disk `\mathbb{D}`.

This is shocking. A square, a star, a blob‚Äîthey are all "the same" as a circle from the perspective of Complex Analysis.

Section 6

Python: Conformal Visualization

Python Mapping Geometries 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Visualizing the Joukowsky Transform (Airfoils)
# f(z) = z + 1/z

def joukowsky(z):
    return z + 1.0/z

# Define a circle in the Complex plane
# Shifted circle maps to an airfoil shape
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 200)
# Center slightly off (-0.1, 0.1) creates camber + thickness
center = -0.1 + 0.1j
# Radius must capture the singularity at z=1 or pass near it
radius = 1.15
circle = center + radius * np.exp(1j * theta)

airfoil = joukowsky(circle)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.subplot(1, 2, 1)
plt.plot(circle.real, circle.imag)
plt.title("Z-plane (Circle)")
plt.axis('equal')
plt.grid(True)

plt.subplot(1, 2, 2)
plt.plot(airfoil.real, airfoil.imag, 'r')
plt.title("W-plane (Joukowsky Airfoil)")
plt.axis('equal')
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
