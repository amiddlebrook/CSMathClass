Section 1

Vector Spaces Reframed

Recall that a Vector Space $V$ over a field $F$ is a set of vectors that can be added together and scaled by elements of $F$.

Key Properties: 1. Basis: Every vector space has a basis. 2. Dimension: The size of the basis is unique. 3. Linear Maps: Structure-preserving functions between spaces.
Section 2

The Module

A Module $M$ is the exact same concept, but the scalars come from a Ring $R$ instead of a field.

Definition: An R-module $M$ is an abelian group (under addition) equipped with an action $R \times M \to M$.

â€¢ Vector Space: Like rigid Legos. If you have 3 blocks, you have exactly 3 blocks. You can always "divide" by a scalar (if you scale by 2, you can scale back by 1/2). It's structurally perfect.
â€¢ Module: Like wobbly Legos or clay. Since "scalars" (from a Ring) might not have inverses (you can't divide by 2 in Integers), you can get "Torsion".
â€¢ Torsion: Imagine taking a non-zero vector, scaling it by a non-zero number, and it collapses to ZERO. That's weird! It happens in modules (e.g., modular arithmetic). Modules are "Vector Spaces with structural defects."

Why it's harder

Because rings (like $\mathbb{Z}$) don't have division, modules are "messier" than vector spaces:

  • No Basis Guarantee: Not all modules have a basis. Those that do are called "Free Modules".
  • Torsion: You can multiply a non-zero vector by a non-zero scalar and get 0! (e.g., in $\mathbb{Z}_6$, $2 \cdot 3 = 0$).

Examples

Vector Spaces: Any vector space implies a field, so every vector space is a module.
Abelian Groups: Every abelian group is a module over the ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}$.
Scaling $n \cdot v$ just means adding $v$ to itself $n$ times.
Ideal: Any ideal $I \subseteq R$ is a module over $R$.
Section 3

Comparison

Vector Space over Field F

  • Always has a basis
  • Isomorphic to $F^n$
  • No torsion elements

Module over Ring R

  • Basis not guaranteed
  • Can be "finitely generated" but look very different from $R^n$
  • Can have torsion
Section 4

Applications

Linear Codes

In coding theory, a linear code is just a subspace (or submodule) of a finite vector space. Parity check matrices generate these submodules.

Homological Algebra

Used in Algebraic Topology to count "holes" in shapes. The "homology groups" computed are actually modules over $\mathbb{Z}$.
