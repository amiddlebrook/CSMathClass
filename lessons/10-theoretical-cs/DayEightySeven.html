Section 1

The Quantum Countdown

Even though large-scale quantum computers don't exist yet, the threat is real today. Adversaries can capture encrypted traffic now (**Harvest**) and wait for a quantum computer to exist in 10-20 years to **Decrypt** it.

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) refers to cryptographic algorithms (usually public-key) that are thought to be secure against a cryptanalytic attack by a quantum computer.

Section 2

Why Current Crypto Fails

RSA relies on the hardness of **Integer Factorization**. ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography) relies on the **Discrete Logarithm Problem**. Shor's algorithm provides an exponential speedup for *both*.

To survive, we need problems that even a quantum computer cannot solve efficiently. These problems often involve finding short vectors in high-dimensional lattices.

Section 3

Lattice-Based Cryptography

A lattice is a periodic set of points in $n$-dimensional space. The "Hard" problem is the **Shortest Vector Problem (SVP)**: Given a basis for a lattice, find the shortest non-zero vector.

In high dimensions (e.g., $n=500$), this appears to be extremely difficult for both classical and quantum computers.

Section 4

Learning With Errors (LWE)

LWE is the foundation of modern PQC. It's like solving a system of linear equations, but with a tiny amount of "noise" added to each result.

Without the noise, solving $Ax = b$ is easy (Gaussian elimination). With noise, finding $x$ becomes practically impossible unless you have a "trapdoor."

Section 5

The NIST Finalists

NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) has been running a global competition to standardize PQC. The winners include:

  • **CRYSTALS-Kyber:** For key encapsulation (replacing Diffie-Hellman/RSA).
  • **CRYSTALS-Dilithium:** For digital signatures.
  • **Falcon:** Another signature scheme.
Section 6

Python: A "Toy" LWE Implementation

Let's simulate how LWE adds noise to make linear system solving difficult.

Python Toy LWE 
import numpy as np

# Modulo
q = 13

# Secret 's'
s = np.array([5, 2])

# Generate random 'a' vectors
n_samples = 5
A = np.random.randint(0, q, (n_samples, 2))

# True results b = A * s mod q
b_true = (A @ s) % q

# Add small noise (Error 'e')
e = np.random.randint(-1, 2, n_samples)
b_noisy = (b_true + e) % q

print("Linear Equations with Noise:")
for i in range(n_samples):
    print(f"{A[i][0]}*s1 + {A[i][1]}*s2 \u2248 {b_noisy[i]} (mod {q})")

# Without noise, 2 samples solve s perfectly.
# With noise, even with 5 samples, it's a 'learning' problem.
