Section 1

The Map of Discrete Math

Discrete Mathematics is not just one topic; it is the study of mathematical structures that are countable (distinct and separated). Unlike Calculus, which deals with continuous change, Discrete Math deals with the "on/off" reality of computers.

Why did we study these?

  • Sets & Combinatorics: How much data is there? (Capacity)
  • Graphs & Trees: How is that data linked? (Network/Hierarchy)
  • Logic & FSMs: How do we process that data? (Software/Hardware)
  • Number Theory: How do we protect that data? (Cryptography)
The Big Picture: Every line of code you write is a transformation of a state inside a finite state machine, involving logic gates that process Boolean values, stored in data structures representable as graphs or trees.

Review: Data Structures

1. Sets & Relations

The foundation of Database theory (SQL).

The Inclusion-Exclusion Principle:
$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$$ If you don't subtract the intersection, you double-count the people who like both Pizza and Tacos.

2. Combinatorics (Counting)

Type Order Matters? Repetition? Formula
Permutation Yes No $$P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$
Combination No No $$C(n, r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$
Arrangements Yes Yes $$n^r$$

3. Graphs & Trees

Graph Properties

  • Handshaking Lemma: Sum of degrees = 2 × Edges.
  • Complete Graph ($K_n$): Every node linked to every other.
  • Planar: Can be drawn without crossing edges.

Tree Properties

  • Edges: Exactly $V - 1$.
  • Binary: Max 2 children per node.
  • Traversal: BFS (Queue) vs DFS (Stack).
Section 3

Review: Logic & Algorithms

1. Boolean Algebra Cheat Sheet

How code "decides."

De Morgan's Laws:
1. `not (A and B)` is `(not A) or (not B)`
2. `not (A or B)` is `(not A) and (not B)`

Implication ($P \implies Q$)

Only False if the promise is broken ($T \implies F$).

P Q Result
T T T
T F F (Broke promise!)
F T T (Vacuously true)
F F T (Vacuously true)

2. Finite State Machines (FSMs)

DFA Definition: A 5-tuple $(Q, \Sigma, \delta, q_0, F)$ consisting of States, Alphabet, Transitions, Start State, and Accept States.

FSMs are the implementation of Regex. If you can draw it as a circles-and-arrows diagram, you can code it as a `switch` statement or a lookup table.

Section 4

Review: Security & Number Theory

How we turned Math into a Shield.

The RSA "Recipe"

  1. Pick two large primes $p$ and $q$.
  2. Compute modulus $n = p \times q$.
  3. Compute $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$.
  4. Choose encryption key $e$ (usually 65537).
  5. Compute decryption key $d$ (inverse of $e \pmod{\phi(n)}$).
The Hidden Difficulty: Factoring $n$ back into $p$ and $q$ takes billions of years for large numbers. That time gap is why your credit card is safe online.

Hashing vs Encryption

  • Hashing: One-way (Mod bucket). Good for passwords and data integrity.
  • Encryption: Two-way (Key). Good for messages and files.
