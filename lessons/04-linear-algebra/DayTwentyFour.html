Section 1

The Definition

The "Axle" Analogy

Imagine a spinning globe suitable for a classroom. Every point on the globe is moving (New York is spinning, London is spinning).
But there are two points that are stationary: The North and South Poles.
The vector connecting them is the Eigenvector (The Axis of Rotation).
The scalar $\lambda$ tells you if the globe is stretching along that axis (if $\lambda=1$, it's just spinning).

$$ A \vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v} $$

• "Eigen" comes from German for "Own" or "Characteristic".
• It's the vector that keeps its own direction.

Finding Them: The Characteristic Equation

We want $A\vec{v} - \lambda \vec{v} = 0$, or $(A - \lambda I)\vec{v} = 0$.
For a non-zero solution $\vec{v}$ to exist, the matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ must be Singular (squish space).

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$
Section 2

Deep Dive: Google PageRank

Google didn't just count links. They modeled the web as a fluid dynamic system.

1. The Stochastic Matrix

Imagine 1 billion web surfers clicking randomly. We define a matrix $M$ where $M_{ij}$ is the probability of moving from page $j$ to page $i$.
Key Property: All columns sum to 1 (Probabilities).

2. The Steady State

Let $\vec{p}_t$ be the vector of "Where everyone is right now".
After one click, the new distribution is $\vec{p}_{t+1} = M \vec{p}_t$.
Eventually, people settle into a "Steady State" where $\vec{p}_{steadystate} = M \vec{p}_{steadystate}$.

Wait... look closer.

$M \vec{v} = 1 \cdot \vec{v}$
The Steady State is just the Eigenvector with Eigenvalue $\lambda = 1$!
Finding the most important websites is literally just finding this vector.

Section 3

Deep Dive: PCA

Data often has redundant features. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) finds the "true" underlying variation.

$$ C = \frac{1}{n-1} X X^T $$

This is the Covariance Matrix. It describes how every variable relates to every other variable.

The Recipe

1. Calculate the Covariance Matrix $C$.
2. Find its Eigenvectors.
3. The Eigenvector with the largest Eigenvalue points in the direction of maximum variance (the "Main Concept").
4. The second largest is orthogonal to the first, and captures the next most important trend.

Section 4

Spectral Graph Theory

Can we "hear" the shape of a graph? The eigenvalues of a graph tell us about its structure.

The Laplacian Matrix ($L$)

Defined as $L = D - A$ (Degree Matrix - Adjacency Matrix).

The Fiedler Vector: The eigenvector corresponding to the second smallest eigenvalue ($\lambda_2$).
It is used to cluster data. It magically separates the graph into two optimal halves.

Section 5

Quantum Perspective

In Quantum Mechanics, the state of a particle is a vector $|\psi\rangle$.

Schrödinger's Equation
$$ \hat{H} |\psi\rangle = E |\psi\rangle $$

• $\hat{H}$: The Hamiltonian Operator (Matrix).
• $|\psi\rangle$: The Wavefunction (Eigenvector).
• $E$: Energy (Eigenvalue).

Every measurable quantity in the universe (Energy, Momentum, Spin) is just an eigenvalue of some operator.

Section 6

Python: From Scratch

We could just call `np.linalg.eig`, but let's implement Power Iteration (the core of PageRank) and PCA manually to see how they work.

Python Power Iteration & PCA 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def power_iteration(A, num_simulations=100):
    # Random initial vector
    b_k = np.random.rand(A.shape[0])
    
    for _ in range(num_simulations):
        # Calculate the matrix-by-vector product Ab
        b_k1 = np.dot(A, b_k)
        
        # Calculate the norm
        b_k1_norm = np.linalg.norm(b_k1)
        
        # Re-normalize the vector
        b_k = b_k1 / b_k1_norm
        
    return b_k

# 1. PageRank Style (Dominant Eigenvector)
M = np.array([
    [0.9, 0.1],
    [0.1, 0.9]
])
dominant_vec = power_iteration(M)
print(f"Dominant Eigenvector: {dominant_vec}")
# Should settle on direction [0.707, 0.707] (Equal probability)

# 2. Manual PCA
np.random.seed(42)
# Generate correlated data
x = np.random.randn(100)
y = 2 * x + np.random.randn(100) * 0.5
X = np.vstack([x, y])

# Covariance Matrix = XX^T / (n-1)
cov_matrix = np.cov(X)
print(f"\nCovariance Matrix:\n{cov_matrix}")

# Get Eigenvectors
eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(cov_matrix)
print(f"\nPrincipal Component 1: {eigenvectors[:, 0]}")

# Visualize
plt.scatter(x, y, alpha=0.5)
origin = [0, 0]
plt.quiver(*origin, *eigenvectors[:,0], color='r', scale=3, label='PC1')
plt.quiver(*origin, *eigenvectors[:,1], color='b', scale=3, label='PC2')
plt.axis('equal')
plt.legend()
plt.show()
Section 7

Summary

  • Eigenvectors are the "Axles" of the matrix. They keep their direction.
  • Eigenvalues are the "Stretch Factors" along those axles.
  • PageRank uses the Dominant Eigenvector ($\lambda=1$) of the Web Graph to rank pages.
  • PCA uses the Eigenvectors of the Covariance Matrix to find the "Main Concepts" in data.
  • Spectral Theory: The eigenvalues of a graph (Laplacian) reveal its connectivity and clusters.
  • Quantum Mechanics: Energy levels are just the eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian matrix.
