Section 1

Gaussian Elimination

The "High School Method" (Substitution) works for 2 variables but explodes in complexity for larger systems.
Gaussian Elimination is the systematic algorithm used by computers to solve linear systems of any size.

The Goal: Triangular Form

We want to eliminate variables column by column until the matrix forms a triangle (Row Echelon Form). Once triangular, we can solve the last equation easily and work backwards.

The 3 Elementary Row Operations:

These operations change the equations but preserve the solution set (the intersection point remains the same).

  1. Swap Rows: Change the order of equations (e.g., to put a non-zero number in the pivot position).
  2. Scale Row: Multiply an entire equation by a non-zero constant ($3x=6 \implies x=2$).
  3. Row Combination: Add a multiple of one row to another ($R_2 = R_2 - 3R_1$). This is the "elimination" step.
Why Swap Rows? (Partial Pivoting)

Computer arithmetic is not perfect. If a pivot is very small (e.g., $0.000001$), dividing by it amplifies errors.
Strategy: Always swap rows to put the largest possible absolute value in the pivot position. This minimizes numerical error.

Phase 1: Forward Elimination

We use the top-left entry (Pivot) to kill all entries below it.
Then we move to the next pivot (diagonal down) and repeat.
The result is an Upper Triangular Matrix ($U$).

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{2} & 1 & 1 \\ 4 & -6 & 0 \\ -2 & 7 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 = R_2 - 2R_1} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{2} & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & \mathbf{-8} & -2 \\ 0 & 8 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$
Phase 2: Back Substitution

Once we reach the bottom ($z = \text{value}$), we substitute it up into the previous equation to find $y$, and so on.

Section 2

The Matrix View (Ax = b)

We write systems as a single matrix equation $A \vec{x} = \vec{b}$.

The Augmented Matrix $[A|b]$

To perform row operations efficiently, we glue $b$ to the side of $A$. Any operation on $A$ must also happen to $b$.

$$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 3 & 1 & 9 \\ 1 & 2 & 8 \end{array} \right] $$
The Two Pictures
Row Picture (Intersection)

Each row is a plane. We are looking for the single point where all planes satisfy their equations simultaneously.

Column Picture (Combination)

Are the columns of A capable of reaching the target vector b?
$$ x_1 \vec{v}_1 + x_2 \vec{v}_2 + \dots = \vec{b} $$ If b is outside the "Span" of the columns, there is no solution.

Possible Outcomes

Just like lines in 2D, planes in 3D can interact in 3 ways:

  • One Unique Solution: Planes intersect at a single point (Matrix is Invertible/Full Rank).
  • No Solution (Inconsistent): Planes are parallel but distinct (e.g., $0=1$ row).
  • Infinite Solutions (Dependent): Planes intersect along a line or coincide entirely. We have "Free Variables".
Inverse vs. Solve

Mathematically, $\vec{x} = A^{-1} \vec{b}$.
Computationally, we almost NEVER calculate $A^{-1}$ explicitly.
We perform Gaussian Elimination on $[A|b]$ directly to get $\vec{x}$. It is numerically more stable (less rounding error).

Section 3

CS: Computability & Stability

Why don't we just find the formula for the inverse and use it?

⚠️ The Complexity Trap ($O(N^3)$ vs $O(N!)$)

Cramer's Rule (using determinants) has factorial complexity $O(N!)$. For a 20x20 matrix, it would take millions of years.
Gaussian Elimination is $O(N^3)$. For 20x20, it takes milliseconds.

Numerical Stability

In computers, numbers are not exact (floating point). If you divide by a tiny pivot (e.g., $10^{-9}$), errors explode.
The Condition Number measures this. If high, the matrix is "Ill-Conditioned" (nearly singular).

Section 4

Worked Examples

Example 1: A Unique Solution

Solve the system:

$$ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 5 \\ 2x - y &= 4 \end{aligned} $$

Step 1: Augmented Matrix

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & | & 5 \\ 2 & -1 & | & 4 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Eliminate $x$ in Row 2 ($R_2 = R_2 - 2R_1$)

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & | & 5 \\ 0 & -3 & | & -6 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 3: Back Substitution

From $R_2$: $-3y = -6 \implies y = 2$.
Substitute into $R_1$: $x + (2) = 5 \implies x = 3$.
Solution: (3, 2).

Section 5

Application: Chemical Balance

Balancing reactions is just solving a homogeneous linear system.

Combustion of Propane

$$ a C_3H_8 + b O_2 \to c CO_2 + d H_2O $$

This gives a system $Ax=0$. We find the smallest integer solution (in the Null Space).
Result: 1, 5, 3, 4.

Resistor Circuit (Kirchhoff)

We have 3 loops with currents $I_1, I_2, I_3$. The sum of voltage drops is zero.

$$ \begin{cases} 10 - 2I_1 - 4(I_1 - I_2) = 0 \\ -4(I_2 - I_1) - 2I_2 - 2(I_2 - I_3) = 0 \\ -2(I_3 - I_2) - 4I_3 = 0 \end{cases} $$

Rearranging gives a clean $3 \times 3$ system $RI = V$.

Section 6

Python: Intersection Viz

Let's visualize the intersection of two lines (system of 2 equations).

Python Solving & Plotting 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# System:
# 1x + 1y = 5  => y = 5 - x
# 2x - 1y = 4  => y = 2x - 4

# 1. Analytic Solution
A = np.array([[1, 1], [2, -1]])
b = np.array([5, 4])
sol = np.linalg.solve(A, b) # [3. 2.]

# 2. Visualization
x = np.linspace(0, 5, 100)
y1 = 5 - x
y2 = 2*x - 4

plt.plot(x, y1, label='x + y = 5')
plt.plot(x, y2, label='2x - y = 4')
plt.plot(sol[0], sol[1], 'ro', label='Solution (3,2)')

plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.title("System Geometry: Intersection")
plt.show()

# 3. Benchmark: Inversion vs Solve
import time

N = 2000
BigA = np.random.rand(N, N)
Bigb = np.random.rand(N)

# Method 1: Solve (Fast & Stable)
t0 = time.time()
np.linalg.solve(BigA, Bigb)
print(f"Solve time: {time.time()-t0:.4f}s") # ~0.15s

# Method 2: Inverse (Slow & Unstable)
t0 = time.time()
# np.linalg.inv(BigA) @ Bigb  # Uncomment to test (slower!)
print(f"Inv time: {time.time()-t0:.4f}s") # ~0.60s (3-4x slower)
Section 7

Summary

The Universal Solver

Gaussian Elimination (Ref + Back Sub) is the standard algorithm for solving $Ax=b$. It is $O(N^3)$, causing scalability issues for huge systems.

Inverse vs Solve

Never compute $A^{-1}$ explicitly in code. Use `np.linalg.solve(A, b)`. It is faster and more stable.

Geometric intuition

Solving a system is finding the intersection of hyperplanes. If they don't intersect, no solution. If they overlap, infinite solutions.
