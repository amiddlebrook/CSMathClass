Determinants & Inverses

Section 1

The Determinant (Scaling Factor)

Forget the formula ad - bc. Think geometry.

Geometric Meaning

Before the transform, take a unit square (Area = 1).
After the transform, measure the area of the new shape.
The Determinant IS that area.

Examples:
• $\det(A) = 2$ means the matrix doubles the area of everything.
• $\det(A) = 1$ means area is preserved (Rotation/Shear).
• $\det(A) = 0$ means the area is squished to Zero (Flat line).

Negative Determinants (Orientation)

What if $\det(A) = -1$? Area can't be negative!

A negative sign means the space has been flipped inside out (like a Mirror Reflection).

Chirality (Handedness):
If you transform your right hand with a negative determinant matrix, it becomes a left hand.
This is crucial in Physics and 3D Graphics (Normals facing in or out).
Section 2

The Inverse & Singular Matrices

The Inverse Matrix ($A^{-1}$) undoes what $A$ did. If $A$ rotates 90 degrees right, $A^{-1}$ rotates 90 degrees left.

$$ A \cdot A^{-1} = I $$ (Applying a transform and then un-applying it leaves you with Identity / No Change).
Singular Matrices (The Black Hole):
If $\det(A) = 0$, the matrix squishes space into a lower dimension (e.g., 3D cube $\to$ Flat Sheet).
You cannot "unsquish" a flat sheet back into a cube. Information is destroyed.
Thus, Singular Matrices have NO Inverse.
The Rank:
The "Rank" of a matrix is the dimension of the output.
For a $3 \times 3$ Matrix:
• Rank 3: Full Volume (Invertible).
• Rank 2: Flat Plane (Singular).
• Rank 1: Line (Singular).
• Rank 0: Point (Zero Matrix).
Section 3

Worked Examples: By Hand

While computers do this instantly, knowing the manual method builds intuition.

1. Determinant of a 2x2

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ \det(A) = ad - bc $$

Example: $A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

  • $ad = 3 \times 2 = 6$
  • $bc = 1 \times 4 = 4$
  • $\det(A) = 6 - 4 = 2$ (Area doubles).

2. Inverse of a 2x2

Swap $a$ and $d$, negate $b$ and $c$, divide by determinant.

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} $$

Example: Using the matrix above ($\det=2$):

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -2 & 1.5 \end{bmatrix} $$
Section 4

CS: Computational Reality

In school, you learn Cramer's Rule to solve linear systems Using determinants. NEVER USE IT.

Cramer's Rule

Uses Determinants.

Complexity: $O(N!)$ (Factorial!)

For $N=20$, takes millions of years.

Gaussian Elimination

Row Reductions ($LU$ Decomp).

Complexity: $O(N^3)$.

Standard and fast.

Determinants are great for theory (checking invertibility), but terrible for calculation.

Section 5

CS Application: Data Science

In Data Science, we often deal with "Covariance Matrices".

  • If the Determinant is widely positive, your features are independent (good info).
  • If the Determinant is near zero, your features are highly correlated (redundant info).
  • This is key for dimensionality reduction (PCA).
Section 6

Python: Transformations & Viz

Numbers are dry. Let's see the "squish" in action using Matplotlib.

Python Visualizing Linear Transforms 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def plot_transform(matrix, title="Transform"):
    # 1. Create a Grid of points
    x = np.linspace(-1, 1, 10)
    y = np.linspace(-1, 1, 10)
    X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
    points = np.vstack([X.flatten(), Y.flatten()])
    
    # 2. Apply Matrix Transform
    new_points = matrix @ points
    
    # 3. Plot
    plt.figure(figsize=(6, 6))
    plt.scatter(points[0,:], points[1,:], alpha=0.3, label="Original")
    plt.scatter(new_points[0,:], new_points[1,:], c='r', label="Transformed")
    plt.xlim(-3, 3); plt.ylim(-3, 3)
    plt.grid(True)
    plt.axhline(0, color='black'); plt.axvline(0, color='black')
    plt.legend()
    plt.title(f"{title} (Det={np.linalg.det(matrix):.2f})")
    plt.show()

# Example: Shear Matrix
Shear = np.array([[1, 1], [0, 1]])
plot_transform(Shear, "Shear Matrix")
Section 7

Summary

  • Geometric View: Determinant = Scaling factor of Area/Volume.
  • Orientation: Negative Det means "flipped" (Mirror).
  • Singular: Det = 0 means flattening. Information is lost.
  • Rank: The dimension of the output space. Full Rank = Invertible.
  • Computation: Avoid Cramer's Rule. Use Gaussian Elimination ($O(N^3)$).
