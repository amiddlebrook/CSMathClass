Section 1

Visualizing the Grid

A "Linear" transformation has two rules:
1. Grid lines remain parallel and evenly spaced.
2. The Origin (0,0) stays fixed.

Why 'Linear'?

It preserves lines. If you transform a line, it stays a line. It doesn't curve (like $y=x^2$).

Section 2

Basis Vectors (The Architects)

The standard basis vectors define our coordinate system:
$\hat{i} = [1, 0]$ (Right)
$\hat{j} = [0, 1]$ (Up)

The Column Interpretation

The columns of Matrix A tell you exactly where $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ land.

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \color{red}{3} & \color{blue}{0} \\ \color{red}{1} & \color{blue}{2} \end{bmatrix} $$

• Column 1 (Red): $\hat{i}$ lands at $[3, 1]$.
• Column 2 (Blue): $\hat{j}$ lands at $[0, 2]$.
Once you know where these two go, every other vector just follows them ($v = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j}$).

Section 3

The Visual Dictionary

Let's build a library of standard 2x2 matrices and see what they do to the "Unit Square".

1. Identity (Do Nothing)

$$ I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

$\hat{i}$ stays at $[1, 0]$. $\hat{j}$ stays at $[0, 1]$. Total boredom.

2. Uniform Scale (Zoom)

$$ S = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Everything doubles in size. Area quadruples ($det = 4$).

3. Reflection (Mirror)

$$ F = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

$\hat{i}$ flips to $[-1, 0]$. $\hat{j}$ stays put. This creates a mirror image across the y-axis.

determinant = -1 (Orientation flip).

4. Shear (Italics)

$$ H = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

$\hat{i}$ stays at $[1, 0]$.
$\hat{j}$ moves to $[1, 1]$.
The square becomes a parallelogram (like pushing a stack of books).
Area is preserved ($det = 1$).

5. Rotation (90° CCW)

$$ R = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

$\hat{i}$ rotates to $[0, 1]$ (becomes old $\hat{j}$).
$\hat{j}$ rotates to $[-1, 0]$ (becomes negative x).

Section 4

Composition ($AB \neq BA$)

Applying two transformations is equivalent to multiplying their matrices. But remember: Functions digest form right to left!
$f(g(x))$ means $g$ happens first.
$ABx$ means $B$ happens first.

Hand Calculation

To compute $A \cdot B$, take the Dot Product of Rows of A with Columns of B.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ae+bg & af+bh \\ ce+dg & cf+dh \end{bmatrix} $$

Think of it as: Matrix A acts on each column of Matrix B separately.

Order Matters!

Rotating then Shearing is NOT the same as Shearing then Rotating.
Try it with your phone: Rotate 90° Z, then 90° X. Now swap. You end up facing different ways.
Mathematically: $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$.

Python Verify Non-Commutativity 
import numpy as np

# Rotation (90 degrees)
R = np.array([[0, -1], [1, 0]])

# Shear
H = np.array([[1, 1], [0, 1]])

print("Rotate then Shear (H @ R):")
print(H @ R)

print("\nShear then Rotate (R @ H):")
print(R @ H)

# They are different!
Section 5

Kernel & Dimensional Collapse

The Image (Output)

The "Range" of the matrix function.

All possible places you can land.

Usually the whole 2D plane, unless you get "squished".

The Kernel (Null Space)

The set of vectors that map to Zero.

If $\det(A) = 0$, the matrix squishes the plane onto a line (Rank 1) or point (Rank 0).

The stuff that gets squished away is the Kernel. It's "Lost Information".

Determinant Deep Dive

The Determinant isn't just a number; it's the Area Scale Factor.

Meaning of Det(A) values:
  • Det = 1: Area preserved (Rotation, Shear).
  • Det = 2: Area doubles (Scaling).
  • Det = 0: Area becomes zero (Collapse to line/point). Not invertible!
  • Det = -1: Area flips (Reflection). Concepts like "Left" and "Right" swap.
Section 6

Application: Computer Graphics

Homogeneous Coordinates

A 3x3 matrix can Scale and Rotate 3D points, but it cannot translate (move) them. Why? Because $linear$ transforms fix the origin ($0 \to 0$).

The 4x4 Trick

To move the camera, Game Engines use 4D matrices! We add a "w" coordinate (usually 1).
This allows "Shear in 4D" to act as "Translation in 3D".

The Pivot Trick (Translate-Rotate-Translate)

To rotate an object around its center (not the origin), we compose three matrices:
1. Translate center to origin ($T^{-1}$).
2. Rotate ($R$).
3. Translate back ($T$).
Combined Matrix: $M = T \cdot R \cdot T^{-1}$.

Texture Mapping

To paint an image onto a 3D triangle, GPUs solve the Inverse Problem: "For this pixel on the screen, which part of the texture image belongs here?"

Section 7

Python: Grid Warp

Let's visualize exactly how a matrix warps a coordinate grid.

Python Visualizing Transforms 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def plot_grid(matrix):
    # Create a grid of points
    x = np.linspace(-5, 5, 20)
    y = np.linspace(-5, 5, 20)
    X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
    
    # Flatten positions to (2, N)
    original_points = np.vstack([X.flatten(), Y.flatten()])
    
    # Apply Matrix Transform: A * v
    transformed_points = matrix @ original_points
    
    # Reshape back to grid for plotting
    X_new = transformed_points[0, :].reshape(20, 20)
    Y_new = transformed_points[1, :].reshape(20, 20)
    
    plt.figure(figsize=(8, 8))
    plt.title(f"Transform: {matrix.tolist()}")
    
    # Plot distorted grid lines
    for i in range(20):
        plt.plot(X_new[i, :], Y_new[i, :], 'b-', alpha=0.3) # Horizontal lines
        plt.plot(X_new[:, i], Y_new[:, i], 'b-', alpha=0.3) # Vertical lines
        
    # Plot Basis Vectors (Where i and j land)
    origin = np.zeros(2)
    i_hat = matrix @ np.array([1, 0])
    j_hat = matrix @ np.array([0, 1])
    
    plt.arrow(0, 0, i_hat[0], i_hat[1], color='r', width=0.1, label='i_hat')
    plt.arrow(0, 0, j_hat[0], j_hat[1], color='g', width=0.1, label='j_hat')
    
    plt.axis('equal')
    plt.grid(True, alpha=0.1)
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()

# Example: Shear Matrix
M = np.array([
    [1, 1],
    [0, 1]
])
plot_grid(M)
Section 8

Summary

  • Matrices are Actions: They are functions that warp space.
  • Columns = Destinations: The columns tell you where basis vectors $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ go.
  • Grid Lines Stick Together: "Linear" means lines remain parallel and the Origin is fixed.
  • Determinant: A single number describing Area Scaling. 0 = Collapse, Negative = Flip.
  • Composition: $AB$ means "Apply B then A". Order matters ($AB \neq BA$).
  • Graphics: Uses 4x4 Matrices (Homogeneous Coordinates) to enable Translation.
  • Inverses: $A^{-1}$ restores the grid. No inverse exists if $det(A)=0$.
