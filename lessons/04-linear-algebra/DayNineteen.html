Section 1

Two Perspectives

Depending on who you ask, a "Vector" is a very different thing. To become a Data Scientist, you must be comfortable shifting between these mental models instantly.

ðŸ”­ Physics View

An arrow with Length (Magnitude) and Direction.

It exists in space, independent of coordinates.

Used for: Velocity, Force, Electric Fields.

ðŸ’» CS View

An ordered list of numbers.

user_A = [25, 50000, 5.9]

Used for: Databases, Features, Audio samples.

The Bridge:
We map the CS "list of numbers" to the Physics "arrow" using a coordinate system.
[3, 2] means "Start at (0,0), go 3 steps Right, 2 steps Up".
This lets us use Geometry (Distances, Angles) to solve Data problems (Similarity, Clustering).
Section 2

Linear Combinations & Span

Most of Linear Algebra boils down to one simple operation: Scaling vectors and Adding them together.

$$ \vec{v} = c_1 \vec{v_1} + c_2 \vec{v_2} + ... + c_n \vec{v_n} $$

This is called a Linear Combination.

The Span

The "Span" of a set of vectors is the set of ALL possible linear combinations you can reach.

Analogy: If you can only walk North and East, your "Span" is the entire North-East quadrant. If you can walk North, East, and South, your Span might be the whole 2D plane.

ðŸŽ¨ Analogy: Printer Colors
Think of vectors as primary colors (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow).
â€¢ Linear Combination: Mixing them. "50% Cyan + 20% Yellow".
â€¢ Span: The entire "Gamut" (rainbow) of colors you can create.
â€¢ If you lose Yellow, your "Span" shrinks. You can no longer make Green.
Section 3

Linear Independence & Basis

If I have vectors $\vec{i}$ (East) and $\vec{j}$ (North), I can reach any point on the map.
What if I add a third vector $\vec{k}$ which points North-East?

It is redundant! I could already go North-East using just $\vec{i}$ and $\vec{j}$. $\vec{k}$ adds no new "directions" to my Span.

Definition: Linear Independence
A set of vectors is Linearly Independent if NO vector in the set can be written as a linear combination of the others.
Mathematically: $c_1 v_1 + ... + c_n v_n = 0$ is only possible if ALL $c_i = 0$.

The Basis

A Basis is the "Goldilocks" set of vectors:
1. Enough to span the space.
2. Not too many (Linearly Independent).

The number of vectors in a Basis is called the Dimension.
â€¢ A Line has dimension 1 (1 basis vector).
â€¢ A Plane has dimension 2 (2 basis vectors).
â€¢ Our Universe has dimension 3 (3 basis vectors).

Section 4

Vector Spaces & Subspaces

A Vector Space is just a fancy name for "a playground where rules of addition and scaling apply". For something to be a Vector Space, it must satisfy notions like:

  • Closure: If you add two items, the result is still in the space.
  • Zero Vector: There's an origin.
  • Inverses: You can go back.
Mind-Bending Concept: Functions are Vectors!
In CS and Math, "Vectors" aren't just arrows.
â€¢ Polynomials: $P(x) = x^2 + 2x + 1$ is a vector $[1, 2, 1]$.
â€¢ Audio Signals: A waveform is just a very long vector of samples.
â€¢ Images: A 1080p image is a vector with $1920 \times 1080$ dimensions.
This is why Linear Algebra is the "Theory of Everything" in CS. It works for Audio, Graphics, AI, and Logic.
Section 5

Vector Norms (L1 vs L2)

The "Magnitude" or "Length" of a vector depends on how you measure it.

L2 Norm (Euclidean)

The straight-line distance (Hypotenuse).

$$ ||v||_2 = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} $$

Use case: Physics, Geometry, Standard Distance.

L1 Norm (Manhattan)

Sum of absolute values (Taxicab distance).

$$ ||v||_1 = |x| + |y| $$

Use case: Sparsity in ML (Lasso Regression).

Why strict Math matters:
In High Dimensions ($1000D$), the "Euclidean" distance becomes meaningless (Curse of Dimensionality).
Often, the simple "Cosine Similarity" (Angle) is much better than Distance.
Section 6

The Dot Product

How do we multiply lists of numbers? We sum the pairwise products.

$$ \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \sum a_i b_i = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + ... $$ Geometric meaning: $$ \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = ||A|| ||B|| \cos(\theta) $$
ðŸ›’ Analogy: The Receipt
Vector A: [Apples, Bananas, Carrots] (Quantities)
Vector B: [PriceA, PriceB, PriceC] (Prices)
Dot Product: Total Bill.
ðŸ’¡ The Intuition:
â€¢ Same direction = Positive (High).
â€¢ Perpendicular = ZERO (No interaction).
â€¢ Opposite = Negative.
Section 7

Cosine Similarity

In Natural Language Processing (NLP), words are vectors. "King" might be [0.9, 0.2] and "Queen" might be [0.85, 0.9]. How do we know they are similar? We look at the ANGLE between them.

Cosine Similarity Formula:
$$ \text{similarity} = \cos(\theta) = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{||A|| ||B||} $$
Range: -1 (Opposite) to 1 (Identical). 0 means Unrelated.
Why divide by length?
If User A rates 1 movie [5 stars], and User B rates 100 movies [5 stars, 5 stars...], the Dot Product would be huge just because B watched more.
Dividing by length normalizes it. We just compare their *taste* (direction), not their volume.
Section 8

The Cross Product (Normals)

The Dot Product returns a Number (Scalar). The Cross Product returns a new Vector.

The Result:
$\vec{A} \times \vec{B}$ is a vector perpendicular to both A and B.
The length is the Area of the parallelogram formed by A and B.

CS Use Case: 3D Graphics. To find which way a triangle is facing (the "Normal"), you take the Cross Product of two of its edges. This tells the lighting engine how to bounce light off it.

# 1. Create vectors

plt.grid()
plt.legend()
plt.title("Vector Addition Visualization")

Python Movie Recommender 
import numpy as np

def cosine_similarity(vA, vB):
    dot_prod = np.dot(vA, vB)
    norm_a = np.linalg.norm(vA)
    norm_b = np.linalg.norm(vB)
    return dot_prod / (norm_a * norm_b)

# Features: [Action, Romance, Sci-Fi, Comedy]
# My taste: Love Action & Sci-Fi
me = np.array([5, 0, 5, 1])

# Movie Database
movies = {
    "Star Wars": np.array([5, 1, 5, 2]),
    "The Notebook": np.array([0, 5, 0, 3]),
    "Matrix": np.array([4, 0, 5, 1])
}

# Find best match
for title, vector in movies.items():
    sim = cosine_similarity(me, vector)
    print(f"Similarity to {title}: {sim:.2f}")

# Output:
# Similarity to Star Wars: 0.98 (Recommended!)
# Similarity to The Notebook: 0.14
# Similarity to Matrix: 0.99 (Best!)
